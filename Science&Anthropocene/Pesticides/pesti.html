Pesticides in the Anthropocene

The story of pesticides began when DDT saved thousands of lives in World War II by killing malaria-carrying mosquitoes as the first “organic” chemical insecticide. Since then, with the progress of chemical technology, various organic compounds have been used in pest control and achieved excellent results. However, are those chemical pesticides in the Anthropocene really so perfect?

Global Effects

Image credit: https://www.researchgate.net/figure/A-schematic-view-of-the-pesticide-cycle-in-an-ecosystem_fig1_339370004

Pesticides can reach many areas through the water cycle and have widespread, global effects. “The heavy use of pesticides in agriculture has resulted in global exposure to these chemicals. Traveling through water, air, and in migrating animals, pesticides can be found in drinking water reservoirs, the atmosphere, on mountain tops, and even in remote areas in the Arctic where they are not used.” This picture shows the process of pesticides reaching other areas through water circulation. The pesticide sprayed on the surface of plant leaves evaporates into the atmosphere together with the water generated by plants and forms clouds. Those polluted clouds will reach other areas through wind drift and generate precipitation, which pollutes the soil and water resources.

Negative impact

01

Target Organisms (Pests)

Image credit: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pesticide_resistance

The widespread use of pesticides will raise the resistance level in the target insect, making the use unsustainable. Before the application of pesticide A, a small percentage of individuals within a pest population developed pesticide resistance through genetic mutation. After applying pesticide A, most non-resistant individuals were killed, and resistant individuals survived and reproduced, creating a population with a higher proportion of pesticide resistance.

After several breeding cycles, almost all individuals in this population developed resistance to pesticide A. At this point, only new pesticide B can kill those pests. However, through the same process, the pest population will gradually develop multiple resistance to pesticides A and B. Since the widespread use of pesticides is no longer sustainable, scholars point out “the great need to manage susceptibility to antibiotics and pesticides as an ecosystem service in order to preserve an operating space of biocide susceptibility in the Anthropocene.”

02

Non-target Organisms

Image credit: https://www.holganix.com/blog/what-is-the-soil-food-web

· Non-specific pesticides can kill non-target insects. When non-specific pesticides are applied to kill an insect pest, they also kill all the other insects exposed to it. This may include useful insects, like bees that are needed to pollinate crops.

· Pesticides that kill insects can threaten the survival of other organisms through the relationship of predation.Currently, in areas of high human activity, insects are under simultaneous threat from several factors, led by pesticides.“Locally and regionally, insects are challenged by additional stressors, such as insecticides, herbicides, urbanization, and light pollution.”After pesticides kill most insects that come into contact with them, insect populations will decline significantly. Since most insects are primary consumers in the food web, they feed on producers and are preyed upon by secondary consumers. Therefore, if the number of insects decreases, secondary consumers such as rats and frogs will be at risk of food shortages, and their populations will also decline. In the same way, the food source of tertiary consumers who prey on secondary consumers will be affected.

03

Human

If people do not clean the food that has been sprayed with pesticides, the pesticides on its surface will hurt human health through the endocrine system. “Emerging data demonstrate that even low— formerly considered 'non-toxic'— concentrations of pesticides can impact health, physiology, reproduction and development through endocrine-disrupting effects.”

In addition, some non-biodegradable pesticides can linger in the environment for a long time, poisoning top carnivores, led by humans, through bioaccumulation. For example, DDT has been a very effective insecticide in killing mosquitoes to reduce the spread of malaria and was widely used from 1945 to 1962. However, the publication of Rachel Carson's book Silent Spring is a wake-up call for humans abusing pesticides in the Anthropocene. Since DDT doesn’t break down, it accumulates in the animal's tissue through bioaccumulation. The higher an animal is on the food chain, the greater the concentration of DDT in its body. As the invention and user of DDT, human beings have become its victims while enjoying its high efficiency in killing pests.

Biological and Physical Control -- Alternatives to Pesticides in the Anthropocene:

Chemical pesticides are indispensable for plant protection, but their harm to other non-target organisms and the environment is also difficult to ignore. Therefore, scientists are trying to replace chemical control with biological control and physical control.
